I am going through an open-source existential crisis. I use open-source projects every day and have been for a long time. Many of my opinions have changed, and I begrudgingly have come to the conclusion that going forward I have no intention of investing my time in any closed-source software. Much like the sentiment I have expressed about [self hosting](https://kieranwood.ca/tech/blog/reluctant-self-hoster/), and the [decline of Microsoft](https://kieranwood.ca/tech/blog/microsoft-please/) in a few other articles, I have not come to this decision lightly. I would like software to be no different than any other product. I like the ability to build kits and put things together, and sometimes I just want to buy a product and let someone deal with it. The main thing I liked was being able to choose the degree to which I engaged with my software. IKEA furniture can be fun, but sometimes I just wanna pay a guy to deliver me a sofa. But modern software is like paying a guy to deliver me a sofa that's leaking gasoline; at some point I know this is going to catch fire.

This realization could not have come at a worse time with the current [state of GitHub](https://damrnelson.github.io/github-historical-uptime/), various open-source debacles ([libre office](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eweMlWewcVg), [MinIO](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=k0K9tA5hEOI), [Booklore](https://www.xda-developers.com/single-maintainer-open-source-ticking-time-bomb/?ref=dailydev), etc.), and how busy I am. If I were back in my 19 y/o self's life, this would be a fun time to waste my life building everything myself. These days I have grass to touch and money to make, so I want things to be more low-maintenance. Unfortunately, this has never been open sources' strong suit...

## Community expansion

Somewhere around 2018 I started noticing a big push for people to start doing open source. It was no longer just a niche hobby, but something I was hearing people talk about outside the compsci world. Partially, I think this is to do with the expansion of data science (and now AI), and webdev. The great thing was a lot of people who weren't "traditional programmers" were now programming, the bad thing is a lot of people who weren't "traditional programmers" were now programming. As is the nature of open source, no one vets things very much. In the past no one really cared about checking if some random project had "good coding standards", just if it worked for what they needed. Most people, honestly, still adhere to this standard. That being said, with this community expansion also came a ton of new people into the tech business space. For someone trying to start a company, the proposition of open-source is very different. From a purely business alignment, open-source is often considered a liability to do yourself, and a useful mechanism to extract free value from other people. 

This may seem like a harsh reading of the situation, but it's also true. If you just care about the economics, this is how open-source functions in relation to your business. Additionally, much of open-source became a poster for how good a coder you are. Projects would now serve to show potential employers how much you know, or give a chance for a large company to hire you to work on your project if they use it. Projects slowly became products. Because of all this, and various other phenomena, open-source projects are now very hard to get a read on. Is the package you're looking at some random person's side project? Or is this just the open-source package used internally at a fortune 500 company and the maintainer is on a full-time salary? Maybe both, maybe neither, maybe somewhere in between. All of this is to say that in the past you could much more easily differentiate high effort projects, and stuff thrown together. Likewise, if you wanted to hack away at a project and make changes, you would just do it. But now, everything is so much more complicated.

If I want to submit patches to a random repo I'm using, there's now often a bunch of paperwork I need to do. Check contribution guides and codes of conduct, and the 45 different ways people specify their preferences. Then the actual process begins: first fork, then make a branch, then submit those changes upstream to update my main once they're added, go back and forth on implementation details, and then I'm finally allowed to have my own changes on my own main branch. Making a fix and submitting a PR can take days of time (if you ever even get a response) between back and forth, or these days proving you're not an AI. All of this "seriousness" means for a lot of projects it's rarely worth it, particularly unless you're going to make a ton of changes. Not to mention the fact that because a bunch of people now know GitHub exclusively for networking, you never know if something is open source to be open source, or just source-available for a resume. If it's the latter they will often never take your patches since many people view it as hurting the perception that they can do everything themselves.

## The Dilema

All of this leads to the important question: If I find a project I like, but something is broken or could be improved, should I rely on someone else and work with them? or just fork it and do what I want? This shouldn't be a hard question. Open source is **open** source. You're allowed to fork anything and do what you want (within the license terms), but somehow because of all the ego and seriousness around everything, it feels... wrong. It feels like to do anything you always have to do it "the right way". This means the old practice I used to do of just grabbing a repo, working on it in my own main branch, and submitting a big PR with my changes, doesn't happen anymore. In fact, I just don't really contribute much anymore externally because if I actually intend to use the library, I have to do all this weird dance to use **my own** changes. A bunch of my projects are just internal tooling I wrote because it's easier than using what already exists. 

In the wake of a lot of the AI slop that exists these days, I can't really trust open source for a whole new reason. This means that ironically, I would rather use old software I know was **most likely** written by people, and not just vibe-coded. The likelihood people adhere to better software standards is actually higher in my experience when they had to do it by hand. There's more pride, care, and interest in the work that took you hours vs mins of slop. Older software is not impervious to this phenomenon; in fact, I got burned recently because the author of Booklore, which was a fantastic book library management app, [decided to nuke the project when people asked him to stop vibe-coding features](https://www.xda-developers.com/single-maintainer-open-source-ticking-time-bomb/?ref=dailydev). But that being said, I would still rather roll the dice on older unmaintained software than AI stuff these days.

This means for me that I want to try to revive projects that are good but have been left to rot. There are tons of good code out there that's just been thrown away, or left to stagnate. I don't blame people for this. People get busy, they get sick of the community, new stuff is more exciting than maintenance, you change jobs etc. There's tons of good reasons a project can rot. I have a few of my own that are as well, so I'm not judging this happening. You certainly don't owe people volunteer work. But, all of this get's back to my previous point. If I want to revitalize a project, I want to just work on it. All of the beaurocracy is something I have no interest in dealing with anymore. If there hasn't been much motion on it besides automated pipelines in years I think I'm just going to start hard forking. What I mean by this is, if there's code that works well, but a dependency issue, or set of small fixes is all it needs to be revitalized, I'm going to start forking and working on my own replacement. This would primarily be for projects where it's not worth starting from scratch, and the changes are mostly addative, or fixes. Essentially forks that I will bring up to my standard, make the changes I need, and move on after my list of changes is done. Micro-forks where the base project is solid enough, but needs sprucing up.

### Drill

This came up recently. I'm doing some work that needs to test the limits of an [http proxy I'm writing](https://kieranwood.ca/tech/blog/usage-aware-caching/), and ran into a ton of issues using my usual first-choice [locust](https://locust.io/). I wanted something simpler, and more portable. After looking into a ton of options I found one I really liked, [drill](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill). It had everything I needed, and the philosophy and approach taken to everything was fantastic. Extensible, performant, binaries that can be pre-compiled, among various other features. So, I ran the install command, and... it didn't work. Apparently OpenSSL was a system-level requirement as a transitive dependency of the version of [reqwest](https://docs.rs/reqwest/latest/reqwest/) being used. This is a pain in the ass on windows. So, I screwed around and finally got it working, I posted my process to an [existing issue](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/124#issuecomment-4185940970) and finished my testing.

![screenshot of comment](/tech/blog/fear-of-forking/original-issue.png)

But, something didn't sit right with me. I actually really liked this project, and wanted to keep using it. But, I knew this install issue would piss me off again in the future. So, instead of being lazy I figured I would submit a quick patch for this issue. I forked, made a commit, and got ready to submit a PR. Frankly if there was a contribution guide I didn't bother reading it because this was a stepping-stone to validate the work I wanted to actually do. Before I could, I ran into a panic on the main thread in my tests. I don't write rust, so I was very worried at this point. Again, I want to like this project, so I decided to dig in and I eventually realized the timeout system was in the wrong units. So, I fixed that bug, and submitted [a somewhat apologetic PR with both changes](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/pull/223). I then realized the issue was highly unlikely to be merged. I was not the first one to fix either of these issues, and [a fix PR](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/pull/166) existed as far back as 2023, with multiple others since then. This project is great, it works so well when everything is setup, and I want to keep using it, but the bureaucracy feeling is rising inside me again. 

I went through the PR's and issues. Everything on there was already done (had a PR), or was pretty trivial. The list was:

1. Move `main.rs` into `lib.rs`, and call it as a `main()` to enable easier lib usage
2. Add command line options to override those in benchmark file [Pull Request #99](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/pull/99), also makes adding benchmark file optional
3. Fix LICENSE file typos and weirdness
4. Update name; Name conflict with several existing drill commands  for DNS [Issue \#160](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/160), [Issue \#181](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/181)
5. Show max request time in statistics [Issue \#188](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/188)
6. Unmaintained repos as dependencies:
    - RUSTSEC-2025-0134: rustls-pemfile is unmaintained [Issue \#203](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/203) (Already fixed in my branch)
    - RUSTSEC-2024-0375: atty  is unmaintained [Issue \#196](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/created_by/196)
    - RUSTSEC-2024-0421: idna accepts Punycode labels that do not produce any non-ASCII when decoded [Issue \#198](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/198)
7. Release builds for all platforms; Currently only supports linux and macOS builds on release because of OpenSSL issues, so with my updates I can add windows as a build target

All of these are very do-able. There are some other larger issues I wouldn't mind tackling eventually as well:

1. Proper documentation site; to help clean up some of the docs, and provide more clarity around how the numbers effect one another
2. [UDP support](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/created_by/196) (requested, but less likely because of complexity)
3. PDF export; Essentially the ability to export results to a PDF to help drive business cases
4. Live-environment; A mini web-app that will let you interactively setup tests, or run specific ones from a set benchmark file
5. Single-command execution; Essentially being able to run it as a single command without the benchmark file against single endpoints
6. Ready-to-run docker image that can be used in a docker compose file to run against microservices and dump the results
7. PGO optimization; Someone mentioned finding [PGO-based optimizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profile-guided_optimization) in [this issue](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/185) using [cargo-pgo](https://github.com/Kobzol/cargo-pgo), and I woudln't mind looking more into this
8. TUI: a command-line way to generate tests since using YAML as a DSL can be a pain in the ass, and the web client won't always be available

I imagine this will all take a fair bit of time, so I want to **know** I can make changes quickly, especially when some changes will rely on other changes to work. In [an existing issue](https://github.com/fcsonline/drill/issues/200) I posted saying I would wait until May 1st to hard fork these changes, to give the author time to respond. But, this is stupid. I have an interest in the project, and I actively need it for my other work, this is the best time to work on it, so I am. I've already started putting together proper formal documentation, and made a bunch of changes. The technical details will be in a later post, but the point is, I'm not waiting. This sort of indecisiveness is what will likely lead to me just never doing this in the first place. But, is there another way...

## Other approaches

There are a few other ways I could have gone about this. I could do the whole song and dance of making all these easy-to-integrate patches. It would take a lot of time, but arguably I should "respect" the original dev. This increases my complexity massively to maintain various branches in various states to make the change, and all the work to help integrate them if the original dev wants them down the road.

In a different approach, there was [a video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=08NqrRQArNw) of someone talking about a related set of issues they were experiencing. For them they went the patch-file route, where they apply patches to projects when they get imported. I have done this for work projects before where the project is so massive that it's untenable to be a sole maintainer. This is nice, but for what I'm doing it doesn't work. Moreover, I don't know that this is the way it should be...

## Wrapping up

Much like I massively appreciate, and want to iterate on the work of the original maintainer, I am also a part of this equation. I am offering a ton of my time to make these changes, and if you want them I also don't have to do a bunch of extra legwork. Both sides need to be considered. Open source means I can do what I want so long as I adhere to the license, and I think that needs to be taken advantage of more. Even with my own projects, if someone takes them, forks them, and makes them better, so long as they adhere to the license, that's their right. If it's an [MIT](https://opensource.org/license/mit) project, it's effectively public domain, they don't even have to submit changes back. Since drill is GPLV3, I just need to continue to allow the source to be available, which I was intending to anyways. If the original dev wants to take my changes and re-integrate them into drill down the road, even better. But for now I am going to stop letting my own psychology stop me from projects, and am going to get working.
