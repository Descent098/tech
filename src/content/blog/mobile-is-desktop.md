Moore's law is dead, and so are most desktops. For those of you who have never heard of this concept, Moore's law states that the number of transistors on a microchip doubles approximately every two years, leading to a reduction in cost, and an increase in compute. It was coined by a co-founder of intel in the mid 60's, and it held true for a long time. But, Moore's law is dead, or at least not as drastic. While this seems like a bad thing the most important thing to remember is that it held for decades. Now that it no longer holds up, how can manufacturers differentiate themselves in the market?

When I got **my** first computer I got a system from costco (yes with a hot dog) that had an [I5-2300](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/52206/intel-core-i52300-processor-6m-cache-up-to-3-10-ghz/specifications.html). 4 cores, 4 threads, 3.1GhZ, 92 watt TDP and 32nm die, with 12GB of DDR3 RAM. At the time this was incredible, and honestly even today this is still a usuable machine. But let's compare that system to my current laptop I'm writing this on, which is a samsung book 3 360. I have an [I7-1355U](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/232160/intel-core-i71355u-processor-12m-cache-up-to-5-00-ghz/specifications.html), let's see how it stacks up:

| CPU | Cores/Threads | Clock Speed | TDP | RAM |
|-----|---------------|-------------|-----|-----|
| [I5-2300](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/52206/intel-core-i52300-processor-6m-cache-up-to-3-10-ghz/specifications.html) | 4/4 | 3.1Ghz | 92W | 12GB DDR3 |
| [I7-1355U](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/232160/intel-core-i71355u-processor-12m-cache-up-to-5-00-ghz/specifications.html) | 10 cores, 12 threads | 5Ghz | 55W | 16GB |

This laptop cost the same amount as my first desktop from costco back in 2013 has 4x (or more) of the performance (according to [cpubenchmark](https://www.cpubenchmark.net/compare/788vs5317/Intel-i5-2300-vs-Intel-i7-1355U)). All while being portable enough to take with me everywhere I go.

This annecdote brings me back to the second part of my original point, desktops are dead. At least they probably should be for most people. I mean this in a few ways, but the most impactful of which is...

## Mobile is Desktop

Mobile chips are largely desktop-level performance these days. The distinction is largely irrelevant. No one blurs this line quite as much as [Minisforum](https://www.minisforum.com/). Much of their business is taking mobile-class systems and turning them into desktops. This approach has lead to some incredible results. When I rebuilt my most recent PC I went with their [BD790i](https://www.minisforum.com/products/minisforum-bd790i) motherboard. This is an ITX motherboard with a Ryzen 9 7945HX, 16 cores, 32 threads, 5.4Ghz clock, and a 75W TDP. This is an insane chip that lives with a full 4070 super and 64GB of RAM in a [Fractal Ridge](https://www.fractal-design.com/products/cases/ridge/ridge/white/), functionally the size of a PS5. This system is enough to run dozens of apps as a server, let alone a consumer desktop. All with a cooler 1/5 the size of my similarly specced [Threadripper 1950x](https://www.amd.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2017-8-10-amd-launches-the-highest-performance-desktop-proce.html) from years ago. This is the same chip you can find in their [MS-A2 mini PC](https://www.minisforum.com/products/minisforum-ms-a2). Unbelieveable compute power at the size of a large hardcover book.

Pushing this theory even further, and in an incredibly surprising move is Apple. The [macbook neo](https://www.apple.com/ca/macbook-neo/) recently released. A laptop built with a phone processor at it's heart. This laptop is cheap (relatively speaking), power efficient, and powerful enough for most people's daily tasks. A literal phone processor in a laptop body is one of the leading products for one of the largest tech firms on the planet. But, what about even further? The next obvious question is, what about the phone in your pocket?

For years now samsung has been including [DeX](https://www.samsung.com/us/support/owners/app/samsung-dex) in their phones. DeX allows you to connect your phone to an external monitor and keyboard, and interface with a desktop-like version of the operating system. Noticing this, companies have taken to producing latop shells that act as docks for your phone, like [LapDock](https://uperfect.com/collections/lapdock) and [NeXDock](https://nexdock.com/). Unfortunately, I don't use Dex. This does not work for my use cases where I need to compile code, and run docker containers frequently. The tooling is just not there to do development work, though to be fair I've never given it a proper shot, so maybe that's not true. That being said, the compute in your phone is already there for most people. 

## Gaming

One of the notable things that was a desktop-only thing for a while was gaming. Until the steam deck it seemed unlikely to get any current-gen games running in a mobile form factor. I remember my friends in high school lugging a 8kg "gaming laptop" to school and claiming it was portable. Now however, the future is here. I have been playing tons of games like Age of Wonders 4, the old Arkham Knight series, and tons of other AAA games on my laptop. Not to mention the steam deck, switch 2, or even my phone.

I recently upgraded to a used Samsung S24 Ultra, and I have been using it to emulate Nintendo Switch games on the train. I used to speedrun [Metroid Dread](https://www.nintendo.com/en-ca/store/products/metroid-dread-switch/), and having it on a phone screen with a controller attached is the most ergonomic way I've found to get back into playing it again on the train. But, my phone can also handle more intense games like [breath of the wild](https://www.nintendo.com/en-ca/store/products/the-legend-of-zelda-breath-of-the-wild-switch/), [tears of the kingdom](https://www.nintendo.com/en-ca/store/products/the-legend-of-zelda-tears-of-the-kingdom-nintendo-switch-2-edition-switch-2/), and even [starlink](https://www.nintendo.com/en-ca/store/products/starlink-battle-for-atlas-digital-edition-switch/). If it has enough power to emulate last-gen hardware, I'm sure with enough tinkering it has enough to run %90 of whatever people need.

## Efficiency

It's no secret things are getting more expensive. As I write this article the political turmoil of the world, and AI datacenters are driving costs of everything through the roof. One such casualty is energy. Power is getting more expensive. As a consequence of this, running a bunch of old high-power hardware is just not as feasible as it was before. Lots of the desktop market is overprovisioned. People waste a huge amount of energy running chips that are just wholly unnecessary. Office workers running 12 core chips with power draw 3x higher than they need for emails and slideshows. This is even worse considering there are systems that are as powerful, but use 1/3 the power. But energy draw is just one side of this coin. 

Another aspect of efficiency is the TDP I mentioned earlier, and what it enables. The TDP is a rough estimate of the amount of heat a chip produces. Lower the TDP, lower the heat. While a chip running cooler is nice in general for longevity and not warming up the room it's in, it comes with another major advantage. When your TDP gets low enough you can start using passive and semi-passive coolers. This means silent, or better, no fans to cool the system. This means quiet operation, but also means the form factors change for what's possible. Physically, coolers just tend to be large. More surface area, more heat dissapation. Removing that physical limitation (or mitigating it) enables much smaller form factors for the same performance. My first PC I got back in 2012 lived in my living room as a media PC for a long time. I tried to replace it with the [intel computestick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_Compute_Stick), and a few other small PC's over the years, but nothing worked properly. Last year the PC started having hiccups, so I looked again and got a [GMTek Nucbox G5](https://www.gmktec.com/products/intel-alder-lake-n97-mini-pc-nucbox-g5). 1.75" x 2.8" x 2.8", a palmable PC half the size of my phone with an [alderlake n97](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/233090/intel-processor-n97-6m-cache-up-to-3-60-ghz/specifications.html) (4 cores, 3.6Ghz, 12wTDP) and 12GB of RAM. Small enough to double-sided tape to the back of a TV. If you step up to the tablet-sized desktop PC space you can cover ~%90 of office workers use cases. 

But let's not stop there, what if we took these chips and put them in situations that were massively performance constrained in the past, like NAS systems. With the ease of self-hosting NAS boxes are getting more popular (or were before hardware got expensive) as an option to drop in some drives and run some apps yourself. The problem is that much of the existing NAS hardware was either consumer grade manufactured e-waste that could only be used as an network drive because the processor was too slow to run any apps, or refurbished server gear so loud you need ear-muffs to start it up. Now, this is far from the case. If you need a bunch of apps and storage on one host then minisforum has their [N5](https://store.minisforum.com/en-ca/products/minisforum-n5-ai-nas) NAS, which is basically the MS-A2 from earlier, but with drive slots. If you need less overall storage, and are fine with coughing up for solid-state, beelink has their [me-mini](https://www.bee-link.com/products/beelink-me-mini-n150), 6 SSD's in a cube the size of a coke can. I used this to run my [immich](https://immich.app/) instance, as well as my book management software, and [romm](https://romm.app/).

## AI

I refuse to use cloud-based LLM providers. While this is a choice that people can choose to make or not, the reality is that AI is just power hungry. Both in compute and memory requirements. In particular AI requires high bandwidth memory, and a lot of it, which is just hard to do. Systems like [STRIX](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sOO4jYh_qFI) have taken an interesting approach. Though it's not "true" high bandwidth memory, STRIX/HALO will allow the system to allocate system RAM to the GPU directly. This helps a lot with capacity, but it's not truely high bandwidth memory. In general it reaches ~256-500GB/s. If you want truly high bandwidth you need to layout a CPU/GPU with the memory directly attached, and a massive [BUS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_(computing)) speed. 

It turns out the company with the least promising AI ventures has some of the best hardware for local AI. Apple's Mac Studio Pro/max models have up to 800GB/s+ and a unified architecture. This makes them incredible for local LLM work. You might assume this would require a huge server, but no. All of that in a tiny package (7.7"x7.7"x3.7"). An inch and change bigger than the height of my phone. If you for some reason need even smaller NVIDIA's [DGX Spark](https://www.nvidia.com/en-us/products/workstations/dgx-spark/) is a mini-pc sized device for local LLM work.

All this is to say that even in this new AI driven, high compute world, mobile-class processing is more than sufficient. While the supplies are low (and expensive), for those willing to shill out there are more and more drop-in AI accelerators and GPU's that can make up the difference. Intel just released it's [ARC Pro B70](https://www.intel.com/content/www/us/en/products/sku/245797/intel-arc-pro-b70-graphics/specifications.html) with 32GB of VRAM on the card. Enough to fit tons of models just in VRAM, and a drop-in card with the same TDP as cards with 1/4 the AI performance. 

## The Point

Everything I've seen leaves me to believe that desktops are dead as we know them (or should be), at least in their traditional bulky form. Beyond artificial market segmentation, and very absolute top-end performance needs, I see little reason for the distinction. The mobile systems of today far surpass the desktops of the past, and in many cases, the desktops of today. So, when you're looking for new devices, for the first time in a long time you should really consider the market carefully. Do you need a separate desktop and laptop? Maybe just a laptop and a [USB 4](https://aoostar.com/collections/egpu-series)/[oculink dock](https://store.minisforum.com/en-ca/products/minisforum-deg1-egpu-dock). Do you need a proper server for your homelab? What about a [Raspberry PI](https://www.raspberrypi.com/), or [zimaboard](https://www.zimaspace.com/) instead.
